Mailchimp

  1. An year ago Mailchimp "pivoted" from an email newsletter application to a service that helps it's customers "Build their brand and sell more stuff". I remember the backlash that they faced on hackernews and other places when they announced their decision but I never found any reading material that considered the move from their point of view. So the question that I'll try to investigate here is:- why an extremely successful email automation company would want move into an e-commerce space? Surely there must be some very compelling reasons.
  2. Looking at their pricing, the free tier is unchanged. They still offer 12K emails per month for 2k subscribers. In the paid plans they have added features like customer segmentation that allows their customers to sell products to a particular demographic. To further demonstrate their ecommerce prowess they advertise features like the ability to follow up after purchases, send triggers on shopping events using their ecommerce ready api and a tight integration with popular ecommerce platforms like shopify and magento.
  3. Although they offer an api to automate the reach for online sellers my gut says that the UI based rules would be far more popular. I feel that it's a very bad investment to write code for a third party service/api that can be changed at the drop of a hat.
  4. Social marketing features like selling on facebook and instagram also sound appealing but my objection remains the same. A small business owner bets on reliability. His business is his livelihood. With these big companies things change, frequently. So if I base my ecommerce business to sell excursively on facebook/twitter/what not and if they suddenly say "oh this third party selling thing is not working out for us we'll have to close the shop sorry fellow" I'll be left stranded. Although this objection is mostly pointless in the context of mailchimp for the moment because they only give you tools to make ads, however its not to improbably to consider that facebook and twitter will add a "shopping cart" someday in their features list and allow users to sell products directly from their website. How would mailchimp or online stores evolve then?
  5. On the other hand social media is a great place to increase your outreach. Build an audience. Interact with your fans online but sell in your own shop, not in theirs.
  6. Overall I feel that it is still the old mailchimp that I knew, it's only the copy that has been changed to attract new kinds of customers, maybe because email marketing services are popping out left right and center so they have to do something to differentiate themselves. I'm sure there would have been a lot of changes to their product and code as well but I'm most happy to discover that, the universal favorite ancient monkey instinct, that we all love so much is still intact. On that note I think it would be appropriate to sign off and eat a banana.

  2. Title: Ecommerce software and shopping cart platform (sounds very generic to me but I guess they would have tested a few different headlines and settled on the one that lead to most conversions)
